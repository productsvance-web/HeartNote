# 03 — Caregiver Education Material: CHF Institutional Survey

**Purpose.** This document surveys authoritative CHF caregiver/patient education from the major U.S. institutions HeartNote will reference and mirror. It captures the exact language, thresholds, and frameworks these institutions use, so HeartNote's in-app education, alert copy, and cardiologist-facing reports align with what caregivers already see in printed handouts, hospital discharge packets, and cardiology-clinic waiting rooms.

**Scope.** Cleveland Clinic, Mayo Clinic, Penn Medicine, American Heart Association (AHA), Heart Failure Society of America (HFSA), AHRQ, CMS, plus adjacent primary-source material from MedlinePlus, Kaiser Permanente, American College of Cardiology (ACC), Family Caregiver Alliance (FCA), and AARP where their content is the most caregiver-actionable.

**Citation rule.** Every threshold, number, or framework claim is footnoted to a source URL.

## 1. The Green / Yellow / Red "Zones" Framework

The zones framework is the single most important caregiver-facing tool in CHF. It translates decompensation risk into a traffic-light system a non-clinician can use at home. HeartNote's red-alert logic must map 1:1 onto these published thresholds so our copy matches what the cardiologist already told the caregiver in clinic.

### 1.1 Origin and adoption

There is no single "inventor" of the CHF zone tool in the literature, but it was popularized through hospital-to-home transition programs in the 2000s and is now distributed by virtually every major U.S. health system. The American College of Cardiology's Hospital-to-Home (H2H) initiative hosts a widely-reused "Zones to Manage Heart Failure Worksheet" developed with Qualidigm, used as a template nationwide. [1]

### 1.2 Cleveland Clinic version (U.S.)

Source: [Cleveland Clinic — Heart Failure Zones](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/departments/heart/patient-education/recovery-care/heart-failure/heart-failure-zones) [2]

- **Green Zone ("All Clear")** — "Symptoms are absent or mild, and stable." Weight is stable, **within 4 pounds of your dry weight**. No new shortness of breath, fatigue, swelling, or chest pain. [2]
- **Yellow Zone ("Caution" — call doctor/nurse)** — "Gain or lose 4 or more pounds from your dry weight." New/worsening shortness of breath with activity or at night. New/worsened swelling in feet, ankles, legs, or stomach. More tired than usual, decreased energy. Dizziness lasting "more than a minute." Change in appetite (less hunger). New or worsened cough. [2]
- **Red Zone ("Emergency" — ER or 911)** — "Struggle to breathe or are short of breath while sitting still." "New or worsening chest pain." "Confused or cannot think clearly." [2]
- Daily requirements: weigh daily, know your dry weight, take all medications, check for swelling, **"low-sodium diet" with "maximum of 2,000 mg of sodium per day,"** exercise with doctor clearance. [2]

### 1.3 Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi version (international, © 2017)

Source: [Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi — Heart Failure Zones](https://www.clevelandclinicabudhabi.ae/en/health-hub/health-resource/diseases-and-conditions/heart-failure-zones) [3]

Same structural framework with both metric and imperial units: weight threshold of **1.8 kg (4 pounds)** from dry weight for yellow zone. Adds "Increased nighttime urination" and "Dry cough" to the yellow-zone list. Red-zone list identical: struggle to breathe at rest, new/worsening chest pain, confusion. Directs calling 999 (UAE emergency number). Copyright 2017. [3]

### 1.4 Kaiser Permanente version

Source: [Kaiser Permanente — Learning About Heart Failure Zones](https://healthy.kaiserpermanente.org/health-wellness/health-encyclopedia/he.learning-about-heart-failure-zones.abp3162), last reviewed October 2, 2025. [4]

Kaiser's yellow zone uses a **stricter, earlier trigger** than Cleveland Clinic:
- "More than 2 to 3 pounds in a day or 5 pounds in a week" triggers yellow zone. [4]
- Red zone includes the distinctive phrase **"You cough up pink, foamy mucus"** (acute pulmonary edema sign). [4]
- Red zone also lists irregular/rapid heartbeat and standard heart-attack symptoms (chest pressure, sweating, nausea, jaw/back/neck pain). [4]

### 1.5 MedlinePlus / NIH (federal reference standard)

Source: [MedlinePlus — Heart failure, home monitoring](https://medlineplus.gov/ency/patientinstructions/000113.htm), reviewed 7/14/2024. [5]
Source: [MedlinePlus — Heart failure, discharge](https://medlineplus.gov/ency/patientinstructions/000114.htm), reviewed 7/14/2024. [6]
Source: [MedlinePlus — Heart failure, fluids and diuretics](https://medlineplus.gov/ency/patientinstructions/000112.htm), reviewed 7/14/2024. [7]

- "Contact your provider if your weight goes up by **more than 2 to 3 pounds (1 to 1.5 kg) in a day or 5 pounds (2 kg) in a week**." [5][7]
- Daily weighing: "Weigh yourself every morning on the same scale when you get up -- before you eat but after you use the bathroom." [6][7]
- Sodium guidance: "Your health care provider may ask you to limit your daily sodium intake to **1500 to 2300 mg per day**." [7]
- Fluid: "As your heart failure gets worse, you may be asked to limit fluids to **6 to 9 cups (1.5 to 2 liters) a day**." [6]

### 1.6 AHA Self-Check Plan

Source: [AHA — Self-Check Plan for HF Management (PDF)](https://www.heart.org/-/media/files/health-topics/heart-failure/hf-symptom-tracker.pdf) [8]

AHA's Self-Check Plan uses an identical green/yellow/red structure. AHA's Lifestyle Changes page (reviewed June 16, 2025) gives the caregiver-facing trigger to report weight gain: **"Two to three pounds in one day for several days in a row"** OR **"Five or more pounds in one week."** [9]

### 1.7 The threshold HeartNote should adopt

The conservative federal-reference rule (MedlinePlus, AHA) is **>2–3 lb in 24 hours OR >5 lb in 1 week**. Cleveland Clinic's "4 lb from dry weight" is a simpler single-number rule. Kaiser and AHA both use the 2–3 lb / 5 lb form. HeartNote's red-alert logic should default to the MedlinePlus / AHA numeric thresholds (widely recognized) and let the user set a personalized "dry weight" so we can compute both (a) the delta-from-dry-weight (Cleveland style) and (b) the 24 hr / 7 day rolling delta (AHA/MedlinePlus style). Trip on whichever fires first.

## 2. Cleveland Clinic — Full Caregiver-Facing Material

Cleveland Clinic maintains one of the most complete consumer-facing CHF education hubs. The landing structure is [Heart Failure Recovery Care](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/departments/heart/patient-education/recovery-care/heart-failure), branching into Living With HF, Zones, Medications, Diet, Exercise & Activity, Managing HF Guide, and FAQs & Resources. [10]

### 2.1 Living With Heart Failure

Source: [Living With Heart Failure](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/departments/heart/patient-education/recovery-care/heart-failure/living-heart-failure) [11]

Daily self-care items stated verbatim:
- "Weigh yourself every day. Know your dry weight." [11]
- "Call your heart failure doctor or nurse if, at any time, you weigh four pounds or more than your dry weight." [11]
- "Take all of your medications every day. Your heart failure medication plan is designed to keep your heart as healthy and strong as possible." [11]
- Exercise target: "Walking or other aerobic types of exercise for about **30-45 minutes of total exercise...five days per week**." [11]
- Stop-the-workout rule: "If you become short of breath when exercising, stop and take a break." [11]
- Pulse alarm line: contact doctor for heart rate exceeding **"120 beats per minute at rest."** [11]

### 2.2 Heart Failure Diet

Source: [Cleveland Clinic — Heart Failure Diet](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/departments/heart/patient-education/recovery-care/heart-failure/diet) [12]

- Sodium limit: **"The usual limits for a sodium-restricted diet are between 1,500 and 2,000 milligrams (mg) per day."** [12]
- Fluid: "Ask your doctor about how much fluid you can have." (Cleveland does not publish a universal daily fluid number — consistent with current evidence that fluid restriction is individualized.) [12]
- Cleveland flags frozen entrées exceeding **600 mg sodium per serving** as to-avoid. [12]
- Cereals should contain **less than 140 mg sodium per serving**. [12]

### 2.3 Unique Cleveland tools

- Cleveland Clinic's Zones page is hosted as a **named clinical tool** in the patient portal, not just a handout. It is referenced by many community hospitals in their own discharge packets.
- Cleveland maintains branded recipes at `clevelandclinic.org/HeartHealthyRecipes` and directs readers to `Healthydiningfinder.com` for sodium-aware restaurant planning. [13]

- Source: [Mayo Clinic — Heart Failure: A Guide for Managing Symptoms and Living Well (PDF)](https://mcforms.mayo.edu/mc0200-mc0299/mc0277-09.pdf) [17]

### 3.2 Mayo emphasis points

- **Why sodium matters, in Mayo's caregiver-accessible framing:** sodium "makes tissues act like a sponge that holds onto water," and this extra retention on top of CHF-driven retention "makes it harder for water pills to work." [15] This framing (sponge analogy) is extremely useful for HeartNote's in-app education because it's memorable and mechanistic.
- **Daily weight instruction:** weigh at the same time each morning, before breakfast and after urinating; write it down to bring to visits so the healthcare team can adjust medications and potentially prevent hospitalization. [16]
- **Restaurant rule:** stick with fresh fish, beef, and vegetables; avoid chicken/pork because they are "often injected or brined with salt." [15]
- **Emergency framing:** Mayo states an emergency-medicine physician's direct line: "It's generally better to err on the side of caution and seek emergency care in almost all cases." [18] HeartNote should consider mirroring this permissive-to-911 tone rather than over-gatekeeping.

Source: [Mayo Clinic Press — When to seek help for chest pain](https://mcpress.mayoclinic.org/healthy-aging/when-to-seek-help-for-chest-pain/) [18]

### 4.1 What Penn emphasizes

Penn's patient-facing material is lighter on numeric thresholds than Cleveland Clinic's — Penn routes patients into individualized care plans rather than publishing universal home-care numbers. Direct quotes:
- Exercise: "Exercise is good for most people with heart failure. It can make your heart and lungs stronger." [19]
- Sodium: "Sodium can lead to swelling and aggravate heart failure symptoms." Delivered via nutritionist-led label-reading education. [19]
- Smoking: "Smoking is especially dangerous for people with heart failure." [19]
- Mental health framed as a parallel care track alongside sleep-apnea evaluation. [19]

### 4.2 Unique Penn content

- **Advanced Heart Care at Home (AHCAH)**: high-risk HF patients get in-home visits and telehealth nurse "coaching cues via telephone" when readmission risk signals appear. Telehealth nurses flag teaching gaps and recontact the patient. [21] This is a caregiver-adjacent model HeartNote's AI trend detection functionally mimics at software scale.
- Penn's public-facing page does not provide a zone tool — if HeartNote plans to cite "Penn" in marketing copy for any specific numeric threshold, it should reference Penn's individualized-plan framing rather than universal numbers.

### 5.5 AHA Questions-to-Ask list (pre-visit prep template)

HeartNote's auto-generated cardiology visit report should mirror the AHA's 11-question template because this is the structure caregivers are already trained to use. [25]

Exact questions:
1. "What's my diagnosis? Is the heart failure mild? Moderate? Severe?"
2. "What should I expect within the next few weeks, months and years?"
3. "What are some specific ways that my daily life will change?" (work, intimacy, chores)
4. "What are the three most important things my family and I can do to manage this condition?"
5. Strategies used by other patients for diet, exercise, smoking cessation.
6. "What will happen if I slip back into unhealthy habits?"
7. Cardiac rehab recommendation, location, frequency.
8. "It's difficult to keep the medication schedule straight. Is there any way we can simplify it?"
9. Medication side effects and alternatives.
10. Insurance coverage gaps for treatment.
11. "If any symptoms seem to get worse or change suddenly, what's the best way for us to contact you?"

### 5.6 Rise Above Heart Failure campaign

Launched by AHA to (a) increase HF awareness, (b) drive patient/caregiver engagement, and (c) reduce HF hospitalizations. Goal set at the launch: cut HF hospitalizations by 10% and raise awareness 10%. [26][27] The caregiver-facing deliverables are the same Self-Check Plan, Medication Tracker, and My HF Guide already catalogued on the Tools & Resources hub. [22]

Source: [AHA newsroom — Rise Above HF launch](http://www.multivu.com/players/English/7635151-aha-rise-above-heart-failure/) [27]

### 5.7 AHA Scientific Statement: Family Caregiving for Individuals With Heart Failure

Source: [Kitko L, et al., *Circulation* 2020;141:e864–e878](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000768), published online April 30, 2020 (print June 2, 2020). PMID 32349542. [28][29]

Summary key points (per ACC's [10 Points to Remember](https://www.acc.org/latest-in-cardiology/ten-points-to-remember/2020/05/14/14/56/family-caregiving-for-individuals-with-heart-failure) summary [30]):
1. "As the number of HF patients increases, the number of available caregivers is expected to decline due to change in family size and composition." [30]
2. Caregivers support daily activities, emotional needs, and healthcare navigation. [30]
3. "Due to the undulating course of HF, caregiver roles and responsibilities constantly change." [30]
4. "Early integration of palliative care has been shown to improve quality of life for both patients and caregivers." [30]
5. Caregivers "incur substantial financial losses." [30]
6. Financial costs disproportionately affect older adults, women, and minorities. [30]
7. Caregivers face "higher physical and psychological health risks." [30]
8. "There have been few high-quality randomized controlled trials" for caregiver-burden reduction. [30]
9. "Studies evaluating use of technology in supporting HF patients and their caregivers are lacking." [30]
10. "Lack of financial incentives for healthcare providers to directly support caregivers forms a major barrier." [30]

**Strategic implication for HeartNote.** Point 9 is the academic equivalent of a green light: the AHA itself calls out the caregiver-technology evidence gap. HeartNote fills that gap directly for CHF.

### 7.1 What AHRQ emphasizes

AHRQ's framing is less "tell the caregiver what to do at home" and more "tell health systems how to involve the caregiver." The useful-for-HeartNote pieces:

- **Teach-Back technique.** Caregivers should repeat back instructions to clinicians in their own words to verify understanding. [40]
- **"Be Prepared to Be Engaged" toolkit** to help caregivers arrive at appointments ready to participate. [40]
- **Transitional care interventions** that reduce CHF readmissions combine caregiver education (including self-titrating diuretics when permitted), medication reconciliation, coordination with outpatient providers, future-care arrangements, and symptom monitoring. [43] These are exactly the five buckets HeartNote's feature set maps onto.
- **Technology opportunity** (cited by AHRQ itself): "telehealth to connect patients and providers for postdischarge follow-ups, digital companion services, mobile applications to check and track nutrition specific to the patient's restrictions, and monitored medication boxes." [43]

### 8.1 What a caregiver needs to know about CMS/Medicare

- **Chronic Care Management (CCM) services** — if the parent has 2+ serious chronic conditions expected to last at least a year, Medicare may pay for a healthcare provider's help coordinating care. Providers prepare a written care plan "for you or your caregiver" explaining what care is needed and how providers will coordinate it. [44] This is something caregivers can actively request from the cardiologist's billing office.
- **Cardiac rehab Medicare coverage for CHF** — covered for stable chronic HF with **LVEF ≤ 35%** and **NYHA class II–IV** symptoms, on optimal HF therapy for at least 6 weeks, with no major cardiovascular hospitalization or procedure in the past 6 weeks or planned in the next 6 months. [48]
- **C-SNPs** — Medicare Advantage plans tailored to people with specific chronic conditions including heart failure. Most Medicare beneficiaries don't know these exist. [47] HeartNote's affiliate/commission stack during AEP (Oct–Dec) should account for this.

## 9. Dietary guidance consolidated

### 9.1 Sodium targets — where the numbers come from

The most caregiver-cited number, **"less than 2000 mg/day,"** traces to the AHA/ACC/HFSA heart failure guidelines and is echoed by Cleveland Clinic, AHA, MedlinePlus, and most hospital discharge packets. [2][12][38]

Exact published ranges:
- Cleveland Clinic: **"maximum of 2,000 mg of sodium per day"** (Zones page) [2]; **"1,500 and 2,000 milligrams (mg) per day"** (Diet page) [12].
- MedlinePlus: **"1500 to 2300 mg per day."** [7]
- Mayo: Sodium-restricted but no fixed number; emphasizes label-reading. [15]

**Caveat to note in-app.** The 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA guideline graded the evidence for aggressive sodium restriction as weak; clinical practice has softened from "the lower the better" to "avoid high-sodium foods, individualize the number with your cardiologist." [38] HeartNote should state the 1500–2000 mg range while making clear the exact target is doctor-set.

### 9.2 Fluid targets — where the numbers come from

- MedlinePlus: **"6 to 9 cups (1.5 to 2 liters) a day"** as CHF worsens. [6]
- 2013 ACC/AHA guideline: 1.5–2.0 L/day fluid restriction in stage D HF. [7]
- Cleveland Clinic: no universal number — "Ask your doctor." [12]
- **Recent evidence update** (March 2025): [ACC press release](https://www.acc.org/About-ACC/Press-Releases/2025/03/30/12/24/Limiting-fluid-Intake-May-Not-be-Needed-for-Some-People-with-Heart-Failure) — fluid restriction may not be needed for all HF patients. [49] HeartNote should treat fluid limits as doctor-individualized, not a universal app-default.

## 10. Activity and cardiac rehab guidance

### 10.1 What patients are told

- Cleveland Clinic: **30–45 minutes total aerobic activity, 5 days a week**; clear with doctor first; stop if short of breath. [11]
- AHA: "Regular, moderate-intensity physical activity can help your heart get stronger." Walking, raking leaves, climbing stairs, playing sports. Schedule daily. [9]
- Penn Medicine: personalized plans via cardiac rehab team. [19]

### 10.2 Cardiac rehab referral criteria

Source: [2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA Guideline](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001063) [38] and [JACC Expert Panel on Cardiac Rehab for HF](https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2021.01.030) [50]

Cardiac rehab is a **Class 1A** recommendation for HF. Medicare-covered patient profile is spelled out in §8.1 above. Referral is under-used; caregivers should explicitly ask, per AHA's Q7 in §5.5.

### 10.3 Activity red flags

From Cleveland Clinic and AHA: stop and rest if short of breath, lightheaded, or chest-discomforted during activity. [9][11] Resting heart rate above 120 bpm should prompt a call to the clinician. [11]

## 11. Pre-visit prep (the template HeartNote's auto-report must match)

Combining AHA, HFSA, Cleveland Clinic, MedlinePlus, and the academic pre-visit-prompt-list pilot, the cardiology-expected structure for a pre-visit caregiver bring-list is:

1. **Current medication list** — drug, dose, frequency, adherence, missed doses. (Per AHA Q8, MedlinePlus [51], HFSA medications module [32].)
2. **Allergies / intolerances.** [52]
3. **Daily weight log** — same scale, same time, same clothing. Highlight any triggers above the 2–3 lb / 5 lb thresholds. [5][6][7][9]
4. **Symptom timeline** — swelling, shortness of breath, orthopnea (needing extra pillows), exercise tolerance, confusion, appetite, cough. [2][4][5]
5. **Blood pressure + heart rate readings.** [5]
6. **Diet/sodium/fluid adherence notes.** [12]
7. **Questions list** — use AHA's 11-question template. [25]
8. **Prior cardiovascular testing results** (ECG, echo with EF, BNP/NT-proBNP, labs). [52]
9. **Family history** — children, siblings, parents, grandparents. [52]
10. **Psychosocial check** — anxiety, depression, sleep issues, sexual activity concerns. [51]

Source: [Ohio State Wexner Medical Center — Questions for first cardiology visit](https://wexnermedical.osu.edu/our-stories/questions-for-first-cardiology-visit) [52]
Source: [MedlinePlus — Heart failure: what to ask your doctor](https://medlineplus.gov/ency/patientinstructions/000224.htm), reviewed 1/1/2025 [51]

**Product implication.** HeartNote's auto-generated visit report should organize into exactly these 10 sections. The cardiologist's intake flow will recognize the structure instantly.

## 12. Caregiver self-care content

### 12.1 What the authoritative institutions say to caregivers

From AHA [23][24], ACC's summary of the AHA caregiving statement [30], and institutional caregiver pages:

- Caregiver physical and mental health "are important"; a "plan and support" are required. [23]
- "Be supportive, but don't do everything for your loved one." [23]
- "Busy Caregivers Must Care for Themselves" — AHA's standalone resource name is itself the messaging. [24]
- Caregivers "incur substantial financial losses." [30]
- Caregivers face "higher physical and psychological health risks." [30]
- "Early integration of palliative care has been shown to improve quality of life for both patients and caregivers." [30]

### 12.2 Burnout & respite resources caregivers are pointed to

- [AHA Support Network](https://supportnetwork.heart.org) — community connection. [24]
- [Family Caregiver Alliance](https://www.caregiver.org/) — assessment, care planning, direct care skills training, wellness programs, **respite services**, legal/financial consultation vouchers. Toll-free line: 800.445.8106. FCA's CareNav® is a personalized dashboard for navigating caregiving. [53]
- [AARP Caregiving](https://www.aarp.org/caregiving/) — explicit sections on Caregiver Burnout, Respite Care, Getting Paid for Caregiving, Tax Tips for Family Caregivers. Cites "49.5 billion hours of care in 2024." [54]
- AHA's dedicated "What is Caregiver Burnout?" PDF. [24]
- Hospice enrollment is specifically cited in the AHA scientific statement as "an avenue for information, support, and respite for caregivers of heart failure patients." [28]

### 12.3 Tone HeartNote should mirror

The caregiver-facing institutional voice has three consistent features:
- **Permissive** rather than prescriptive ("It's generally better to err on the side of caution" [18]; "With proper planning and support, caregiving can be rewarding" [23]).
- **Binary action prompts** — "call your doctor" vs. "call 911" — with explicit thresholds that don't require the caregiver to interpret.
- **Explicit caregiver-as-a-person framing** — the caregiver is not just the patient's helper; their own mental/physical/financial well-being is named and resourced.

HeartNote's red-alert copy, visit-prep copy, and in-app education copy should adopt all three.
